Economic Growth and Fighting Climate Change are Not Mutually Exclusive

(We Graham do it all!)

Last summer, Senator Lindsay Graham, Republican from South Carolina, said, “I don’t want to be lectured about what we need to do to destroy our economy in the name of climate change.” (New York Times 2022). Well, Senator Graham, neither do we - because, that’s not what we have to do.

In 2016, the United States had a GDP of $49,725 and a total ecological footprint (human resource demand in global hectares) of 8.22 - one of the highest in the world, behind only Australia and Qatar. However, there are several other countries with an equivalent (or higher) GDP and a lower total ecological footprint. Sweden comes in with a GDP of $59.381 and a footprint of 7.25. Ireland and Austria have GDP similar to the US and footprints nearly 3 global hectares less. Switzerland and Norway also have similarly low footprints, and GDPs even higher than the US. But do the people in these countries with lower ecological footprints enjoy a high standard of living?
Yes, they do. This is measured by the WHO’s Human Development Index (HDI), which is comprised of three metrics for wellbeing: health, knowledge, and standard of living (WHO). While HDI can be useful for analysis, it is an aggregate metric and therefore does not account for in-country inequalities. In 2016, the United States had an HDI of 0.91 and one of the highest total ecological footprints among countries with similar HDIs. Japan, Italy, Israel, the Netherlands, Cyprus all have HDIs comparable to the United States with significantly lower total ecological footprints. Among GDP peers, Canada is in a similar spot as the United States as Sweden leads with a much lower footprint. Australia lags behind with a higher total footprint.
Total ecological footprint is challenging to compare against other countries because countries are different sizes, as well as populations. When we standardize both country size (biocapacity) and population density we can see that it is possible by example that countries of all different sizes can have a positive biocapacity.
